## My Fundamental Tips for Productivity

Throughout my college years, I worked hard to get the best scores possible in every subject. But as I was finishing my third year, I started asking myself why my grades still weren't where I wanted them. I was aiming for an average of at least 9/10 across all subjects, yet I was stuck at 8.25/10. Why? I was clearly putting in the effort, so I had to be doing something wrong.

So I started digging into study skills, productivity, and better habits. I read every Reddit post and watched every YouTube channel I could find on the subject. What I found — and have been putting into practice ever since — is what I'll detail below.

### 1. Time tracking

The beautiful _time tracking_. Put simply, time tracking is the practice of measuring the time you spend doing something — except you're not the one keeping count. An app does it for you (thanks, programming, for making our lives easier!).

For this, I use the [Toggl](https://toggl.com/) app in its free version, though some people prefer the Pomodoro technique with an app like [Forest](https://www.forestapp.cc/). One article that was essential to understanding how to apply time tracking with Toggl is [If the Pomodoro Technique Doesn't Work for You, Try Flowtime](https://zapier.com/blog/flowtime-technique/).

### 2. Time blocking

_Time blocking_ is, basically, blocking out the hours of your day in advance. When you add blocks to your calendar for the things you plan to do each day, you're time blocking.

I use Google Calendar for this. Here's [The Complete Guide to Time Blocking](https://todoist.com/productivity-methods/time-blocking).

### 3. App blockers

If you think you can resist social media through sheer willpower, congratulations — you're from another planet! Let's be honest: it's incredibly hard not to give in. We tell ourselves “just a little bit,” and then spend hours scrolling Facebook, Twitter, or Instagram without even noticing. To prevent this, there are apps that block other apps — fittingly called _app blockers_. The whole point of using them is to depend less on our limited willpower.

In my case, I use [Cold Turkey](https://getcoldturkey.com/) on my PC and [AppBlock](https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=cz.mobilesoft.appblock&hl=en&gl=US) on my phone — both in their free versions.

### 4. Sleep

Sleeping well is essential for productivity. On one side, I use apps to cut down the blue light hitting my eyes at night — [f.lux](https://justgetflux.com/) on desktop and [Twilight](https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=com.urbandroid.lux&hl=en&gl=US) on mobile. On the other, to keep myself from oversleeping, I use [Alarmy](https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=droom.sleepIfUCan&hl=en&gl=US): to turn the alarm off, it forces me to solve math problems, which leaves me wide awake. As always, I use both in their free versions.

**Note**: For me, seven and a half hours is enough sleep to feel energetic the whole day.

### 5. Training

I'm not sure how strong the scientific evidence is for the mental-health benefits of exercise, but without a doubt I'd recommend it to everyone. My life was full of stress before I started training regularly. And you don't need to pay for a gym to do it: unless you're aiming to become a bodybuilder, training with your own bodyweight — fashionably known as [_calisthenics_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calisthenics) — is more than enough.

### 6. Study

When it comes to studying, I'd recommend the [active recall](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fDbxPVn02VU&ab_channel=AliAbdaal) and [spaced repetition](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Z-zNHHpXoMM&ab_channel=AliAbdaal) techniques. Put simply: a) spaced repetition — do a little every day; b) active recall — the harder you work to recall something, the easier it becomes to remember it next time.

To apply both techniques, there's a wonderful piece of free software: [Anki](https://apps.ankiweb.net/).

### Final words

First, productivity is a means to an end: it brings us closer to our goals and desires. None of us is productive just for the sake of it. Second, productivity is like anything else — it improves with practice and dedication. You don't jump from studying two hours a day to studying ten overnight. And finally, being productive also means finding the way of working in which you feel as comfortable as possible.
