## How I Transitioned From Psychology to Programming

I studied psychology at the University of Buenos Aires (UBA), Argentina. For those who don't know, to study at the UBA you first have to complete the CBC — the “Ciclo Básico Común” (roughly, “Common Basic Cycle” in English). The CBC is a first stage, usually lasting a year, in which you study a handful of introductory subjects to get used to university-level study methods and strategies, with the stated goal of raising students' overall level. Some people argue it's really a filter to keep a lot of people out of university — but that's a topic for another day.

It wasn't until I finished the CBC that I became seriously interested in scientific research. I genuinely wanted to be a researcher. So throughout my degree I was careful about my grades, looked for research teams to join, and studied statistics, methodology, English, and, of course, programming. Nearly two years before graduating, I started programming in the [R statistical programming language](https://www.r-project.org/). For those who don't know, R is one of the best tools for data science, alongside [Python](https://www.python.org/). I started with R because it's the most widely used tool in academia, and because good R books were easy for me to find. My first programming book was [Data Science for Psychologists](https://bookdown.org/hneth/ds4psy/), by Hansjörg Neth.

A few months later, along the same lines, I started learning Python through the courses [Programación en Python - UNSAM](https://github.com/python-unsam/Programacion_en_Python_UNSAM) and [Full Stack Python - Codo a Codo](https://www.buenosaires.gob.ar/educacion/codocodo/el-programa). On top of that, I was working through the [freeCodeCamp curriculum](https://www.freecodecamp.org/), learning HTML, CSS, and JavaScript — mainly because I needed to build a website for a psychology movement, the “Movimiento Estudiantil por la Psicología Científica” (known by its acronym, “MEPC”).

At the same time, my research career was taking off — and then something strange happened. I realized that I loved research itself (my undergraduate thesis was about the replicability crisis and open science practices in psychology), but I hated the bureaucracy around it. I call it “title-itis”: in academia, everything comes down to titles and scholarships. I also realized how much effort it takes just to live decently — at least in Argentina — on very low wages and inside a brutal “publish or perish” culture. That's when I hit a breaking point: what I had spent years preparing for was not what I actually wanted.

But despite the discouragement, not all was lost. I loved programming, and I was good at it. All I had to do was make one small switch: stop reading scientific articles and start reading documentation and code, and building my own projects. And that's exactly what I did. In the end, it wasn't an enormous change, because in many ways the two paths are strikingly similar: both require a lot of studying and research; both demand long hours in a chair; and both reward a genuine interest in logical and abstract thinking.

### Final words

All in all, I can't deny one thing: if I hadn't studied psychology, I might never have discovered programming. I really like scientific research, but I prefer working as a programmer, where my real skills count for more than certificates and titles. And this is just a personal preference — I'm not saying academia isn't valuable. Without science, we would be nothing as humanity. I'm only saying that the academic system isn't for me. The way I see it, I made one small switch: I went from studying science with job expectations and treating programming as a hobby, to treating programming as my job and science as my hobby.
